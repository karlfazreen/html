A customer navigates a <a class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off eb" data-term="programming" href="https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/programming" data-type="EB">programming</a> menu via the cable set-top box and makes a selection, available either at no cost or for a charge. The server immediately begins <span id="ref1295289"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/streaming-data-transmission" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">streaming</a> the program. VOD systems may also use a download-based model, in which the program is stored on a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/hard-disk" class="md-crosslink autoxref" data-show-preview="true">hard disk</a> in the set-top box, or they may transmit over the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/Internet" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Internet</a> to a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/technology/personal-computer" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">personal computer</a>. Satellite television services, which broadcast the same signal over an entire service area, cannot accommodate true VOD, though they often offer Internet VOD.</p><!--[P2]--><span class="marker p2"></span><!--[AM2]--><span class="marker AM2 am-inline"></span><!--[MOD2]--><span class="marker MOD2 mod-inline"></span><p class="topic-paragraph">Cable providers experimented with VOD in the 1990s, but the services failed to achieve much success until the next decade, when equipment and bandwidth became less expensive and content providers began allowing more programming to be offered by VOD. By the middle of the decade, VOD had largely supplanted schedule-driven pay-per-view service on cable systems, and by 2010 most television networks were offering many of their programs on VOD.</p><!--[P3]--><span class="marker p3"></span><!--[AM3]--><span class="marker AM3 am-inline"></span><div class="one-good-fact-module">

New development efforts focused on bridging the gap between Internet and television sets, allowing online rental services to compete with cable providers in bringing content to customers’ television screens. Subscription services such as <span id="ref1295287"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Netflix-Inc" class="md-crosslink" data-show-preview="true">Netflix</a> and Amazon Prime allowed users to stream video for a monthly fee.
